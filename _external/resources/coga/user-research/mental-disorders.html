Mental Disorders

Introduction

This user research is based on findings from a comprehensive systematic literature review [1] that was carried out by the BETTER Project to identify the challenges people with mental disorders encounter when using the Web and any measures to remove or reduce these barriers. 11 of the included studies considered more than one mental disorder and 2 investigated a single disorder each. Therefore, challenges and recommended measures to address them are discussed together here. Please see this publication for detailed information on the methods used.

Findings identified challenges faced by people affected by 3 categories of mental disorders: (1) mood disorders, (2) anxiety and stress-related disorders, and (3) schizophrenia, schizoaffective and psychotic disorders. These disorders along with their associated symptoms, treatment and potential challenges on the Web will be discussed in more detail in the next sections focusing on this user group.

All symptoms and limitations in functioning discussed below are based on internationally-accepted and evidence-based classifications. The International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF) [] is a comprehensive and universally-accepted framework to describe functioning, disability and health. The 10th revision of the International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems (ICD) [] is a medical classification list by the World Health Organization (WHO) that describes mental disorders, among other conditions. The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) [] is another commonly used classification for mental disorders and is published by the American Psychiatric Association.

Motivations behind Web usage

The Web is often used as a source of support for people with mental disorders and shows great promise for the reduction of the burden associated with these disorders.

People with mental disorders use the Web for several reasons:

  • Web-based information seeking is common among people with mental disorders [2, 3].
  • Web-based mental health communities are known to supplement traditional mental health services [4] and act as an important factor in encouraging people with mental disorders to seek professional help [5].
  • Web-based treatment (e.g., cognitive behavioral therapy) options are used as they may be as effective as the face-to-face equivalent for reducing depressive symptoms [6-8].
Mood/Affective disorders
Introduction

Moods are essential to how people experience the world. Moods allow people to judge others and everyday situations, be alerted to threats, recognize opportunities and to convey one’s feelings. The fallout when moods become disturbed is profound and accompanied by considerable distress.

Mood disorders typically feature excessive and or persistent periods sadness, joyousness or both.

  • Depression is the term used to denote feelings of intense and enduring sadness.
  • Bipolar refers to intense and enduring periods of joyousness and sadness.
Spectrum of associated symptoms and limitations in functioning
Depression

Individuals affected by this disorder usually experience many of the below features:

  • depressed mood
  • loss of interest and enjoyment and reduced energy leading to increased fatiguability and diminished activity
  • reduced concentration and attention
  • reduced self-esteem and self-confidence
  • ideas of guilt and unworthiness (even in a mild type of episode)
  • bleak and pessimistic views of the future
  • ideas or acts of self-harm or suicide
  • disturbed sleep
  • diminished appetite

In addition to the internationally-accepted and evidence-based classifications discussed in the introduction, the ICF Core Set for depression was used to further describe functioning, disability and health in persons affected by this condition. This core set can be downloaded using the following link – https://www.icf-research-branch.org/download/send/9-mentalhealth/108-comprehensiveandbrieficfcoresetsdepression.

Bipolar

Individuals affected by this disorder usually experience many of the below features:

  • Occasions of an elevation of mood and increased energy and activity (mania or hypomania).
  • Occasions of a lowering of mood and decreased energy and activity (depression).

In addition to the internationally-accepted and evidence-based classifications discussed in the introduction, the ICF Core Set for bipolar disorders was used to further describe functioning, disability and health in persons affected by this condition. This core set can be downloaded using the following link – https://www.icf-research-branch.org/download/send/9-mentalhealth/123-brieficfcoresetforbipolardisorders.

Web accessibility barriers experienced by people with mental disorders
Summary of Web accessibility barriers identified by studies

Operable - Barriers reported by included studies are primarily related to poorly designed navigational elements (e.g., content filters), difficulties with fine motor coordination (e.g., clicking small radio buttons, operating computer mouse, scrolling), poorly designed pages with time limited response forms, too much information and unoptimised components that contribute to slow webpage loading times.

Understandable - Included studies revealed barriers concerning the use of complicated and excessive content (e.g., high reading level, abbreviations and unfamiliar phrasing), distracting and confusing design, and complex and overindulgent website functions (e.g., excessive advertising and complicated purchasing processes).

Perceivable - Identified barriers point to difficulties with reading small font, recognising icons and locating information.

Scenarios and user stories

This section demonstrates how barriers identified by studies and experts may occur when members of this user group engage in Web activities (e.g., banking, shopping).

Depression

Steve is diagnosed with major depressive disorder. He would like to apply for a credit card online. The application process is long and involves several steps. He becomes frustrated, loses interest and does not complete the application. There is no reminder that some of the steps in the process have not been completed so he does not complete the application.

Bipolar Disorder

Alex is diagnosed with bipolar disorder. At times she will experience periods of significant depression. She will also experience periods of hypomania a mild form of mania, marked by elation, racing thoughts and hyperactivity or mania marked by periods of great excitement, euphoria, delusions, and overactivity. Alex is enrolled in an online course. During a hypomanic episode she posts several ideas to the course forum. Shortly after the episode she would like to remove the posts but there is no mechanism for deleting posts.

Social Anxiety

Kris is diagnosed with generalized and social anxiety. She would like to join a social platform through which users can get advice and discuss coping mechanisms for anxiety. The platform requires new users to create a profile however is does not have an option for privacy settings and the profile is available to anyone through a search engine quer.

Recommendations for improving Web accessibility for people with mental disorders
Summary of recommendations from studies

Facilitation measures recommended by studies were summarised into a group of 20 from 59 recommendations and are shown below. These summarised recommendations are not listed or arranged in any particular order. Nine of the summarised recommendations were the result of empirical work and 11 from the expert opinion of researchers.

Derived from empirical evidence
  • Provide instructions on how to change between different page views.
  • Build websites with a minimal number of layers.
  • Provide legible font and perceivable buttons and links.
  • Comprehensively list hyperlinks surrounding a given topic.
  • Allow for personalisation or getting the best fit for the user.
  • Use of different media and technological additions (e.g., reward logo or bookmark functionality).
  • Use attention grabbing and not boring design.
  • Use simple and familiar language.
  • Use an ample number of images and visual aids.
Derived from expert opinion of researchers
  • Provide intuitive navigation and ensure information filters and search functions work.
  • Provide explicit instructions on how to use the site.
  • Use simple and familiar language with no abbreviations.
  • Allow users to progress through the system at their own pace.
  • Use graphics and colours sparingly and meaningfully.
  • Provide several options (e.g., mouse, keyboard arrows, touch screen) to assist users with navigation.
  • Provide resources in video and audio format.
  • Use legible font and sufficiently large buttons.
  • Use a simple design with webpages that are pleasing to the eye and easy to read.
  • Meaningfully group information.
  • Use a minimal amount of content.
